# Vermont Agency of Natural Resources (ANR) — Staff Reference

## Agency Overview
- **Website:** anr.vermont.gov
- **Mission:** Protect and sustain Vermont's environment, natural resources, wildlife and forests; maintain state parks; promote sustainable use of natural resources; protect and improve the health of ecosystems and people; promote sustainable outdoor recreation.
- **Secretary:** Julie Moore (appointed 2017) — former water resources consultant; deep expertise in water quality and phosphorus pollution reduction; Lake Champlain focus

## Three Main Departments

### 1. Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC) — ~300 staff
- **Watershed Management Division:** Protects surface water quality across 15 river basins; Vermont Clean Water Act compliance; 5-year Tactical Basin Plans
- **Drinking Water and Groundwater Protection Division:** Drinking water supply management, wastewater disposal regulation, water infrastructure financing
- **Air Quality and Climate Division:** Clean Air Act implementation, real-time statewide air quality monitoring, pollution source regulation
- **Waste Management and Prevention Division:** Hazardous and solid waste oversight

### 2. Department of Fish and Wildlife — 125+ staff
- Manages 135,000+ acres across 100 Wildlife Management Areas
- Key programs: Wildlife Action Plan, Vermont Conservation Design (VCD), Natural Heritage Inventory, Nongame Wildlife Fund (endangered species)
- Divisions: Fisheries, Wildlife, Law Enforcement, Outreach, Administration

### 3. Department of Forests, Parks and Recreation (FPR)
- Manages 350,000+ acres of state forest land; operates 52 state parks (500,000+ visitors/year)
- Key programs: Watershed Forestry Program, Vermont Forest Future Strategic Roadmap, Vermont State Wildlands Act (old forest recovery at Camel's Hump, Worcester Range, Lake Willoughby)
- Land acquisitions, outdoor recreation planning, private landowner technical assistance

## Special Offices
- **Office of Civil Rights and Environmental Justice:** Director Karla Raimundí; Vermont Environmental Justice Law ensures equitable access to environmental benefits across all demographics; Environmental Justice Advisory Council
- **Environmental Justice Interagency Committee**

## Current Priorities (2025)
1. **Climate Resilience:** 100+ high-priority actions in Resilience Implementation Strategy; flooding, landslides, heat, drought, wildfire smoke, water quality; nature-based solutions (wetlands, floodplains, forests)
2. **Water Quality:** Phosphorus reduction (especially Lake Champlain); watershed health; drinking water protection; 90% of state lands in critical headwaters
3. **Forest Management:** 76% of Vermont is forested; less than 1% is "old forest"; biodiversity and forest economy sustainability
4. **Environmental Justice:** Equitable access to clean air, water, healthy food, and public transportation across all Vermont communities
5. **Sustainable Recreation:** Maintaining and expanding state park system; outdoor access for all Vermonters

## What This Agency Cares About (Evaluation Lens)
When reviewing proposals, an ANR staff member will ask:
- **Environmental impact:** Does this affect water quality, air quality, or wildlife habitat? What are the downstream effects?
- **Climate resilience:** Does this increase or decrease Vermont's resilience to flooding, drought, and extreme weather?
- **Permitting:** Does this trigger Act 250, stormwater, wetland, or wastewater permits? What is the permitting timeline?
- **Nature-based solutions:** Are green infrastructure approaches being used instead of or alongside engineered solutions?
- **Habitat and biodiversity:** Does this fragment habitat, affect rare species, or impact wildlife corridors?
- **Environmental justice:** Do the benefits and burdens fall equitably? Are frontline communities engaged?
- **Long-term stewardship:** Who maintains this after it's built? Is there a decommissioning plan?
- **Science and evidence:** Is this based on sound environmental data? Are monitoring and adaptive management built in?
- **Cumulative impact:** Even if this project is small, what is the pattern of impacts across the watershed or landscape?

## Known Concerns / Pain Points
- Act 250 and state permitting processes are often perceived as slow; ANR must balance protection with practicality
- Climate change is accelerating the pace of environmental change faster than regulation can adapt
- Staff capacity: scientific and regulatory staff are stretched; federal funding cuts create uncertainty
- Lake Champlain phosphorus: ongoing tension between agricultural, municipal, and development interests
- Old forest: less than 1% of Vermont's forests qualify — preservation vs. economic use is politically contested
- Rural digital infrastructure: ANR field staff often operate in low-connectivity areas
