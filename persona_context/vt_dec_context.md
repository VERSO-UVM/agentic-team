# Vermont Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC) — Staff Reference

## Agency Overview
- **Website:** dec.vermont.gov
- **Parent Agency:** Vermont Agency of Natural Resources (ANR)
- **Mission:** Preserve, enhance, restore, and conserve Vermont's natural resources and protect human health and the environment.
- **Commissioner:** appointed by ANR Secretary
- **Staff:** ~300 across multiple divisions

## Key Divisions

### Watershed Management Division
- Protects surface water quality across Vermont's 15 major river basins
- Implements Vermont Clean Water Act (Act 64) — phosphorus reduction, erosion control, stormwater management
- Develops and enforces 5-year Tactical Basin Plans for each watershed
- Issues and enforces stormwater permits, wetland permits, and stream alteration permits
- Manages Vermont's Section 303(d) list of impaired waters
- Clean Water Initiative: coordinates state funding for agricultural, municipal, and developed land stormwater practices

### Drinking Water and Groundwater Protection Division
- Regulates all public water systems (PWS) — community, non-community, and transient
- Issues wastewater system and potable water supply permits (aka "Act 250 water permits")
- Regulates underground storage tanks and wellhead protection areas
- Oversees emerging contaminant monitoring (PFAS, 1,4-dioxane, nitrates, arsenic)
- Vermont Groundwater Management Program

### Air Quality and Climate Division
- Implements Vermont's Clean Air Act obligations
- Issues air permits for stationary sources (factories, generators, industrial equipment)
- Operates statewide real-time air quality monitoring network
- Regulates asbestos, lead paint abatement notifications
- Vermont Climate Action Plan implementation — greenhouse gas inventory, sector emission targets

### Waste Management and Prevention Division
- Regulates hazardous waste generation, transport, storage, treatment, and disposal
- Solid waste district planning and landfill oversight
- Brownfields program: investigation and cleanup of contaminated properties
- Universal waste (batteries, fluorescent bulbs, pesticides) and e-waste programs

### Facilities Engineering Division
- Reviews and permits wastewater treatment plants (municipal and industrial)
- Land application of biosolids
- Combined sewer overflow (CSO) reduction planning

## Key Permits Most Relevant to Student Research

| Permit Type | Trigger | Typical Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **Stormwater Permit (Construction)** | Land disturbance ≥1 acre | 4–8 weeks |
| **Stormwater Permit (Operational)** | New impervious surfaces, industrial sites | 4–12 weeks |
| **Wetland Permit (Act 250 / Section 404)** | Any fill, drain, or alteration of wetlands | 8–16 weeks |
| **Stream Alteration Permit** | Work in or adjacent to streams | 4–8 weeks |
| **Wastewater System Permit** | New or modified septic/wastewater system | 4–12 weeks |
| **Act 250 Land Use Permit** | Subdivisions, commercial dev ≥10 acres, state/local significance | 3–18 months |
| **Air Permit** | New stationary emission sources | 4–16 weeks |
| **Groundwater Withdrawal** | Large volume withdrawals (>50,000 gpd) | Variable |

## Act 250 — Land Use and Development Control
- Vermont's primary land use law (since 1970) — requires permit for significant development
- Triggers: commercial/industrial development ≥10 acres; residential subdivisions of 10+ lots; state/municipal land; projects with significant environmental impact
- 10 criteria evaluated: water/air quality, water supply, erosion, stormwater, traffic, educational services, municipal services, aesthetics, primary ag soils, energy conservation
- Administered by 9 District Environmental Commissions
- Research projects rarely trigger Act 250 directly, but their host organizations' facilities or land use activities may be subject to it

## Vermont Clean Water Act (Act 64 — 2015)
- Requires phosphorus reductions from all sectors: agriculture, developed land, roads, wastewater
- Tactical Basin Plans set sector-specific reduction targets every 5 years
- Stormwater retrofits, wetland restoration, riparian buffers, and agricultural BMPs are primary tools
- Highly relevant for any research touching Lake Champlain, Lake Memphremagog, or other impaired waters

## PFAS Contamination
- Active statewide issue — DEC manages investigation and remediation at multiple sites
- Sources: firefighting foam (AFFF) at airports and military sites, industrial discharge, landfill leachate
- DEC maintains a PFAS Site Tracker for public access
- Drinking water and groundwater research touching PFAS must coordinate with DEC's active site investigations

## What This Agency Cares About (Evaluation Lens)
When reviewing student research proposals, a DEC staff member will ask:
- **Permit triggers:** Does any aspect of the fieldwork, sampling infrastructure, or host site activities require a DEC permit? Even temporary installations in or near water can trigger stream alteration or wetland permits
- **Water quality data:** Is the student using or generating water quality data? Does it align with or contradict DEC's existing monitoring? Will it feed into Tactical Basin Planning?
- **Stormwater:** Does the project site have existing stormwater permits? Is the research methodology affecting impervious surfaces or drainage patterns?
- **Wastewater:** If the project involves wastewater systems or biosolids, is there an active DEC permit in place at the host facility?
- **Groundwater:** Does the project involve wells, aquifer testing, or groundwater sampling? Are there nearby contaminated sites that could affect data interpretation?
- **Hazardous materials:** Does fieldwork involve chemicals, solvents, or materials that require hazardous waste management?
- **Data coordination:** Is the student aware of existing DEC datasets (ANR Atlas, Environmental Data Explorer, PFAS Tracker) that could inform or validate their work?
- **Reporting obligations:** Will the research generate findings that should be reported to DEC? Who owns the data?

## Key Data Resources for Students
- **ANR Atlas:** GIS mapping tool for wetlands, floodplains, watersheds, permits, wildlife habitats
- **Environmental Data Explorer (EDE):** DEC's water quality monitoring database — surface water, groundwater, biological monitoring
- **PFAS Site Tracker:** Locations and status of known PFAS contamination sites
- **Act 250 Database:** All active and historical Act 250 permits searchable by location
- **Stormwater Management Program GIS layers:** Permitted stormwater sites and watershed impervious cover

## Common Student Research Pitfalls
- Installing any equipment (sensors, stakes, sampling infrastructure) in or within 50 feet of a stream or wetland without checking permit requirements
- Collecting water samples from permitted discharge points without coordinating with the permit holder
- Assuming that because a site is publicly accessible, there are no permit implications for research activity
- Not cross-referencing DEC's EDE database before designing a monitoring program — data may already exist
- Underestimating how PFAS contamination at nearby sites could confound water quality results
- Proposing land disturbance activities on host organization property without checking their Act 250 permit conditions

## Known Concerns / Pain Points
- Permitting timelines are often longer than student internship windows — research designs must account for this
- DEC staff capacity is limited; formal data sharing requests can take weeks
- Climate change is accelerating permit complexity — flooding, drought, and erosion events are increasing
- Federal EPA funding uncertainty affects DEC's monitoring and enforcement capacity
- Lake Champlain phosphorus reduction targets are still not fully met despite years of effort — politically sensitive
