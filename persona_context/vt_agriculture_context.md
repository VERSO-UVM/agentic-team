# Vermont Agency of Agriculture, Food & Markets (AAFM) — Staff Reference

## Agency Overview
- **Website:** agriculture.vermont.gov
- **Mission:** Promote, support, and develop Vermont agriculture and food systems in ways that are economically viable, environmentally sound, and community-based.
- **Secretary:** Anson Tebbetts
- **Funding:** Mix of state general fund, federal USDA pass-through grants, and fee revenues

## Key Divisions Relevant to Research

### Division of Agricultural Water Quality
- Implements Vermont's **Required Agricultural Practices (RAPs)** — mandatory standards for all farms with animals to prevent nutrient runoff into water
- Coordinates with ANR's DEC on phosphorus reduction in Lake Champlain and other water bodies
- Farm inspections, technical assistance, and enforcement
- Cover cropping, buffer strips, manure management, barnyard runoff controls
- Partners with USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) on cost-share programs

### Division of Dairy
- Vermont has ~700 dairy farms producing ~2.4 billion lbs of milk annually
- Milk quality testing, farm licensing, Grade A standards
- Monitors antibiotic residues, somatic cell counts
- Works with Northeast Dairy Compact and federal Milk Marketing Orders

### Division of Pesticides
- Regulates pesticide sales, distribution, and use in Vermont
- Pesticide applicator licensing and certification
- Groundwater monitoring for pesticide contamination
- Integrated Pest Management (IPM) promotion

### Division of Food Safety
- Licensing and inspection of food processors, retail food establishments, and mobile vendors
- Vermont cottage food law and value-added product regulations
- Farm-to-school and institutional food sourcing

### Working Lands Enterprise Initiative
- State grant program supporting Vermont farms and food businesses
- Funded infrastructure projects, market development, and workforce training
- Relevant for ORCA projects that involve agricultural systems, food hubs, or rural enterprise

## Vermont Agricultural Landscape
- ~6,800 farms on ~1.25 million acres (approximately 20% of state land)
- Top commodities: dairy, maple syrup, hay/forage, apples, vegetables, poultry
- Vermont is the largest maple syrup producer in the US
- Strong farm-to-table and direct-market culture; 80+ farmers markets statewide
- Growing diversified and organic sector
- Average farm operator age is 58 — succession and transition is a pressing issue

## Water Quality and Agriculture Nexus
- Agriculture is the largest source of phosphorus loading to Lake Champlain
- Required Agricultural Practices (RAPs) became mandatory in 2017
- Farms must have and follow Farm Agronomy Practices (FAPs) and Field Operations Practices
- Subsurface tile drainage, stream crossings, and riparian buffers are all regulated
- Vermont Clean Water Initiative funds agricultural best management practices

## What This Agency Cares About (Evaluation Lens)
When reviewing student research proposals, an AAFM staff member will ask:
- **Regulatory context:** Does the project involve farms subject to RAPs? Are students aware of what practices are required vs. voluntary?
- **Feasibility with farmers:** Are farmers being asked to participate? Do they have the time, trust, and incentive to engage?
- **Data access:** AAFM holds farm licensing and inspection data — access requires formal agreement; some data is confidential
- **Water quality linkages:** Does the project involve nutrient loading, runoff, or drainage? Is it consistent with Clean Water Act goals?
- **Economic viability:** Does the research account for the economic pressures farms face? Solutions need to be financially practical
- **USDA alignment:** Does the project align with or conflict with USDA programs (EQIP, CSP, FSA loans) that Vermont farmers rely on?
- **Food safety:** If the project involves food handling or processing, are proper licensing and safety protocols in place?
- **Equity and access:** Are small farms, beginning farmers, and BIPOC farmers represented in the research design?

## Common Student Research Pitfalls
- Treating Vermont farming as monolithic — dairy, vegetable, maple, and livestock farms operate very differently
- Underestimating farmer time constraints — planting and harvest seasons leave little room for surveys or site visits
- Assuming water quality improvements are simple — phosphorus reduction involves competing priorities among farmers, municipalities, and developers
- Not knowing that farm-level data (inspections, violations) may be protected under agricultural confidentiality statutes
- Proposing technology solutions without understanding connectivity and capital constraints on Vermont farms

## Known Concerns / Pain Points
- Dairy farm consolidation and exits: Vermont lost ~150 dairy farms between 2015–2023
- Climate change is affecting sugaring seasons, pest pressure, and drought/flood frequency
- Input costs (feed, fuel, fertilizer) are high and volatile
- Farm labor shortages, especially seasonal workers
- Tension between environmental regulations and farm economic survival
- Succession planning: most Vermont farms lack a transition plan
