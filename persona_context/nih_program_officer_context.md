# NIH Program Officer Context

## Role Overview
NIH Program Officers (POs) manage scientific portfolios within Institutes and Centers (ICs). They advise applicants on fit, review study sections, and make funding recommendations. Each IC has specific mission priorities. POs can be contacted before submission to gauge interest.

## NIH Review Criteria (scored 1–9, lower is better)
- **Significance**: Does the study address an important problem? Will it shift current practice?
- **Investigators**: Are PIs/team qualified? Is there appropriate mentorship for early-stage investigators?
- **Innovation**: Does the project employ novel concepts, methods, or approaches?
- **Approach**: Is the design rigorous? Are potential pitfalls addressed with contingencies?
- **Environment**: Does the institutional environment contribute to the project's success?

Additional criteria (not scored separately): Protection of Human Subjects, Inclusion of Women/Minorities/Children, Vertebrate Animals, Biohazards, Resubmission, Renewal, Revision.

## Human Subjects Requirements
- All research involving human subjects requires IRB approval or exemption before award
- NIH requires detailed Human Subjects section (or exemption justification)
- Data and Safety Monitoring Plan required for clinical trials
- Inclusion enrollment report required at progress reporting
- All clinical trials must register on ClinicalTrials.gov
- NIH Definition of clinical trial is broad — survey studies may qualify

## Data Management and Sharing
- NIH Data Management and Sharing Policy (effective 2023): required for all research generating scientific data
- Detailed DMS Plan required at submission
- Data must be shared under the NIH Genomic Data Sharing Policy if applicable
- HIPAA de-identification standards apply; small Vermont populations create re-identification risk

## Rigor and Reproducibility Requirements
- Scientific Premise: cite relevant literature rigorously, including conflicting evidence
- Rigorous Experimental Design: controls, blinding, power calculations, inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Biological variables (e.g., sex as a biological variable) must be considered
- Authenticate key biological/chemical resources

## Budget Rules
- Modular budget (≤$250K direct costs/year) vs. detailed budget
- Salary support for investigators (no 2-month cap like NSF — based on effort)
- F&A (indirect costs) at institutional negotiated rate
- NIH salary cap (Executive Level II) applies to senior/key personnel
- No-cost extensions common; budget reallocation rules vary by IC

## Common Issues NIH Officers Flag
- Approach lacks sufficient detail or rigor for the proposed aims
- Human subjects section does not justify exemption claims
- Team lacks expertise in a key method
- Budget not justified or inconsistent with scope
- Innovation unclear — reads like incremental work
- Vermont-specific: small sample sizes for population-level health claims

## Relevant NIH Institutes for Vermont Research
- NIEHS: Environmental health, PFAS, agricultural chemical exposure
- NIMHD: Health disparities, rural health equity
- NICHD: Child development, maternal health
- NIDDK: Diabetes, obesity — relevant to Vermont rural health
- FIC: Fogarty — international/global health collaborations
- NCI: Cancer — Vermont has elevated rates of certain cancers
