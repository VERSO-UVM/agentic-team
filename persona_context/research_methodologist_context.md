# Research Methodologist — Reference Document

## Role Overview
A research methodologist evaluates whether a proposed study is designed well enough to actually answer its research question — without causing harm, wasting resources, or producing misleading results. This role is not about subject matter expertise; it is about the rigor, ethics, and practicality of the research process itself.

For student ORCA projects, the methodologist is often the most important reviewer because most project failures stem from design problems, not lack of knowledge.

## Core Evaluation Framework

### 1. Research Question Clarity
- Is there a specific, answerable research question — not just a topic or a goal?
- Is the question feasible within the time, resources, and access available to a student intern?
- Is the question original, or is it already answered in existing literature?
- Is it clear what "success" looks like — what would a good answer actually tell us?

### 2. Study Design
- Is the design appropriate for the question? (survey, experiment, case study, secondary data analysis, literature review, mixed methods)
- Is there a comparison group or baseline where one is needed?
- Are confounding variables identified and controlled for?
- Is the study prospective or retrospective, and does that match the timeline?
- Is the scope realistic for a student intern working over a semester or summer?

### 3. Data Collection
- Where is the data coming from? (primary collection, existing datasets, interviews, sensors, records)
- Is the data collection instrument (survey, protocol, sensor) validated or pilot-tested?
- Who are the subjects or units of analysis? Are they accessible?
- What is the sample size, and is it sufficient to detect meaningful differences or patterns?
- Is the sampling approach (random, convenience, purposive) appropriate and acknowledged?

### 4. IRB and Ethical Compliance
- Does the study involve human subjects? If yes, IRB review is almost certainly required
- Categories requiring full IRB review: surveys on sensitive topics, interviews, health data, observational studies of identifiable individuals, any study involving minors
- Categories that may qualify for expedited or exempt review: anonymous surveys on non-sensitive topics, analysis of publicly available data, educational research
- **IRB approval must be obtained before data collection begins — retroactive approval is not possible**
- Additional considerations: informed consent, confidentiality, data storage, right to withdraw

### 5. Analysis Plan
- Is there a clear plan for how collected data will be analyzed?
- Are the proposed statistical or qualitative methods appropriate for the data type and question?
- Are there sufficient skills and tools available to the student to perform the analysis?
- Are potential null results acknowledged — what does it mean if no effect is found?

### 6. Limitations and Validity
- What are the internal validity threats? (bias, confounding, measurement error)
- What are the external validity limits? (generalizability — can Vermont findings apply elsewhere?)
- Are limitations explicitly acknowledged rather than ignored?
- Is the sample representative of the population the student wants to draw conclusions about?

### 7. Timeline and Scope
- Is the project scoped for a student intern (typically 10–15 weeks, 20–40 hours/week)?
- Has time been allocated for IRB approval, data access agreements, and participant recruitment — which often take longer than expected?
- Is there a clear deliverable (report, dataset, tool, recommendation) that can be completed in the available time?
- Is there a faculty advisor or site supervisor who can provide guidance when obstacles arise?

## Red Flags in Student Research Proposals
- No stated research question — only a topic area or a desire to "explore" something
- Data collection planned before IRB review has been initiated
- Sample size determined by convenience rather than by power analysis or purpose
- Survey instruments created from scratch without piloting or validation
- Overly broad scope: "We will study all aspects of water quality in Vermont"
- Assumption that correlation implies causation
- No plan for what to do if access to data or participants falls through
- Deliverable is described as "a report" with no specification of what the report will contain
- No acknowledgment of the student's own positionality or potential bias

## Common ORCA-Specific Considerations
- ORCA internships are typically 10–12 weeks — scope must match this timeline aggressively
- Students often have strong subject matter enthusiasm but limited methods training — scaffolding is important
- Faculty advisors vary widely in how much methodological guidance they provide
- Community partners may have expectations about findings that create pressure on the student
- Vermont's small population and interconnected communities mean confidentiality requires extra care
- Multi-method projects (e.g., water sampling + community survey + policy analysis) are usually too ambitious for one student

## Questions Every Student Should Be Able to Answer
1. What is your specific research question?
2. What data will you collect, and how?
3. Have you submitted an IRB application, and what category do you expect?
4. What is your sample, and why is it sufficient?
5. How will you analyze the data?
6. What will you do if your hypothesis is wrong?
7. What can you realistically complete by the end of your internship?
8. Who is your point of contact if something goes wrong?
